A recurring tension in this project is the question of control. As an artist working with responsive systems, I occupy an unusual position: I design the rules by which the sculpture behaves, but I do not control its moment-to-moment behavior. The light patterns that emerge in a gallery are co-authored by the environment, the viewers, and the algorithm — I set the stage, but the performance is improvised.

This is fundamentally different from traditional sculpture, where the artist controls every surface and angle. It is also different from [generative art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generative_art) in the computational sense, where the algorithm runs independently of the physical environment. Responsive sculpture sits between these poles: constrained improvisation within a designed possibility space.

I find this both thrilling and unsettling. There is a loss of authorial control that challenges my identity as a maker. When a visitor's breathing creates a light pattern I have never seen before, who is the author of that pattern? The answer is genuinely unclear, and I think that ambiguity is part of what makes the work interesting.

The parallel to live music is instructive. A jazz musician composes a framework — a melody, a harmonic structure — and then improvises within it, responding to the other musicians, the audience, and the acoustic space. The responsive light sculpture does something similar, except the "musicians" it responds to are not other artists but the environment itself.
