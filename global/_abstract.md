# Background

As a sculptor and installation artist, I have long been fascinated by the interplay between light, space, and the human body. Over the past decade, my practice has increasingly incorporated digital technologies — LED arrays, microcontrollers, and environmental sensors — to create works that respond to their surroundings in real time. This shift from static to dynamic sculpture has opened up new questions about authorship, materiality, and the boundaries of the art object.

Traditional sculpture occupies space passively. A marble figure or a steel construction exists in a fixed relationship with its environment, changing only as the ambient light shifts throughout the day. My interest lies in sculptures that actively participate in their environment: works that sense, compute, and respond. By embedding sensor arrays and programmable LED systems into sculptural forms, I aim to create pieces that exist in a continuous dialogue with their surroundings — responding to temperature, humidity, ambient sound, and the presence and movement of viewers.

This research builds on earlier experiments with reactive installations exhibited at Atelier Solvang (2022) and the Biennale of Digital Arts in Estara (2023), where I first explored the use of capacitive proximity sensors to trigger light patterns in suspended acrylic forms. These early works revealed both the potential and the limitations of responsive light sculpture: while audiences were captivated by the responsiveness, the technical infrastructure was fragile, the calibration process was time-consuming, and the aesthetic vocabulary of the light patterns remained simplistic.

# Inspiration

I draw inspiration from several lineages of art and technology. The Light and Space movement of the 1960s and 70s — artists like Henryk Soler, Maren Lindquist, and Dalia Voss — demonstrated how light itself could become the primary medium of a sculptural practice. Their work showed that perception is not passive reception but active construction, and that light can reshape our experience of architectural space.

More recently, the responsive installations of Kollektiv Lumen, Studio Rainfall's "Threshold Room," and Enar Thorvald's immersive environments have shown how digital technologies can create deeply embodied experiences for gallery visitors. However, I am interested in a more intimate scale — not the spectacular immersion of a large-scale installation, but the quiet responsiveness of an object that seems to breathe with you.

From the technical side, I am inspired by the maker movement and the democratization of embedded computing. Platforms like Luvon Micro, Crestboard, and the growing ecosystem of affordable sensors have made it possible for artists to prototype responsive systems without formal technical training. Yet the gap between a working prototype and a robust, exhibition-ready installation remains significant.

# Research Questions

The central questions driving this artistic research are:

- How can environmental sensor data be translated into aesthetically meaningful light patterns that go beyond simple reactive mappings?
- What sculptural forms best integrate electronic components while maintaining material integrity and visual coherence?
- How does the responsiveness of a light sculpture alter the viewer's relationship to the work — does it enhance contemplation or create distraction?
- Can a modular, portable sensor-light system be developed that is robust enough for touring exhibitions while remaining flexible enough for site-specific adaptation?

# Methodology

This project follows a [practice-based research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Practice-based_research) methodology, alternating between studio experimentation, public exhibition, and critical reflection. Each cycle of making, showing, and reflecting generates new questions and insights that feed into the next iteration.

The technical work proceeds along three parallel tracks, each corresponding to a research topic:

1. **Sensor Integration** — Developing reliable methods for capturing environmental data (temperature, humidity, sound, proximity) and translating it into control signals for lighting systems.
2. **Projection Mapping** — Exploring techniques for mapping programmed light patterns onto three-dimensional sculptural surfaces, including both LED-embedded forms and projected light.
3. **Audience Interaction** — Investigating how viewers engage with responsive sculptures, what behaviors emerge, and how the design of the interaction shapes the aesthetic experience.

# Constraints

Two practical constraints frame the research:

- **Portability**: The system must be transportable for exhibition in different gallery spaces without requiring extensive on-site calibration.
- **Robustness**: The installation must run reliably for the duration of an exhibition (typically 4-8 weeks) without technical intervention.

These constraints are not merely practical — they shape the aesthetic possibilities of the work and force creative decisions about materials, electronics, and interaction design.
