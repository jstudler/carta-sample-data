The audience interaction research raised a question I had not fully considered at the outset: what are the ethical implications of a sculpture that senses its viewers?

The current sensor array captures temperature, humidity, ambient sound level, ambient light, and proximity. None of these are personally identifiable — the sculpture does not know *who* is nearby, only *that* someone is nearby and how far away they are. There is no camera, no facial recognition, no recording of any kind. The data is processed in real time and immediately discarded.

And yet, several visitors at Galerie Meridian expressed discomfort when they learned that the sculpture was sensing them. One visitor asked, "Is it watching me?" Another said, "I feel like it knows I'm here," which was simultaneously the intended artistic effect and a source of unease.

This discomfort is worth taking seriously. We live in an era of pervasive [surveillance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surveillance), and any technology that senses human presence — however benignly — carries associations with tracking, monitoring, and control. The fact that my sculpture uses the same proximity sensors found in automatic doors and hand dryers does not exempt it from these associations.

I have decided to include a brief statement in future exhibition materials that explicitly describes what the sculpture senses, what it does not sense, and that no data is stored. Transparency is the minimum ethical standard, even when the sensing is genuinely minimal.

The deeper question — whether responsive art inherently involves a power asymmetry between the sensing artwork and the sensed viewer — remains open.
