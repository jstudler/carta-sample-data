As this project nears its conclusion, I want to reflect on the disciplinary tension that has shaped the entire endeavor. I am trained as a fine artist with a background in sculpture and material studies, and this project has required me to engage with technical systems — sensors, microcontrollers, code — that sit outside my primary formation.

The theoretical frameworks I brought into this project — phenomenology of perception, relational aesthetics, material agency — were essential for formulating the research questions and interpreting the results. But they could not tell me which sensor to use, how to filter a noisy signal, or why an LED strip fails after three weeks in resin. That knowledge came only from hands-on experimentation, from trial and error, from the stubborn materiality of electronics and polymers.

Conversely, the technical work constantly generated insights that no amount of theoretical reading could have produced. The discovery that temperature is the most aesthetically productive sensor — because of its slowness, not despite it — emerged from studio practice, not from theory. The washi paper diffusion technique was a happy accident during a material test, not the result of a research plan.

I have come to see this interplay not as a problem to be solved but as a productive friction that generates the most interesting work. The best pieces in this project emerged at the boundary between what I planned and what I discovered, between the conceptual and the material. If I had stayed in theory, the sculptures would be coherent but lifeless. If I had stayed in the workshop, they would be technically interesting but conceptually thin.

The practice of [artistic research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artistic_research) itself — with its insistence on both rigorous documentation and creative practice — embodies this duality. It asks me to be both maker and analyst, both inside the work and outside it. This is difficult, but I believe it produces knowledge that neither pure theory nor pure studio practice could generate alone.
