Measured the [impulse response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_response) of three potential exhibition spaces using a balloon pop + MEMS microphone recording at 48kHz. Spaces tested:

1. **Studio (30m², carpet, low ceiling)** — RT60 ≈ 0.3s. Very dry. The sculpture's sound channel picks up direct sounds cleanly but the space contributes almost no character.
2. **Gallery main hall (120m², hardwood, 5m ceiling)** — RT60 ≈ 1.2s. Rich reverb tail. Footsteps and voices create a complex, evolving sound texture that the sculpture responds to beautifully.
3. **Alcove at Galerie Meridian (18m², concrete, 3m ceiling)** — RT60 ≈ 0.8s. Moderate reverb with strong early reflections. The intimate scale means that a single viewer's breathing is audible to the microphone.

## Observations

The gallery main hall produces the most interesting sound-to-light behavior because the reverb tail creates a sustained, slowly decaying input signal. In this space, a single clap produces a light ripple that fades over about a second — the sculpture makes the room's acoustic decay *visible*.

The alcove is better for intimate breath-following because the short reverb and small volume keep the acoustic signal focused. There is less ambient contamination from other visitors.

The studio is too acoustically dead for compelling sound-to-light mapping. The sculpture responds only to direct, deliberate sounds, which breaks the ambient, responsive character I am aiming for.
