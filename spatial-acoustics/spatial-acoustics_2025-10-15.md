The spatial acoustics investigation produced two significant outcomes: the frequency-band mapping technique and a deeper understanding of how gallery architecture influences the aesthetic character of responsive light sculpture.

## Frequency-Band Mapping

The four-band approach (sub-bass → brightness, low-mid → color temperature, mid → spatial spread, high → shimmer) is now the default sound-to-light mapping for all sculptures in this project. It replaces the earlier single-channel approach that reduced sound to a simple loudness value. Multiple test viewers described the multi-band mapping as "natural" and "intuitive," suggesting that it aligns with innate [crossmodal correspondences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crossmodal_correspondence) in human perception.

## Space as Material

The most important conceptual insight from this strand is that the acoustic properties of a gallery space are not merely contextual — they are a *material* of the work. A reverberant hall and a dead studio produce fundamentally different aesthetic experiences with the same sculpture and the same code. This means that site-specific calibration is not just a technical necessity but an artistic act: choosing the time constants and thresholds for a particular space is a compositional decision.

## Open Questions

The feedback loop risk identified in August remains a theoretical concern. The mitigation strategies (fanless Pi, notch filters, noise gate) have been implemented but not yet tested in a multi-artwork exhibition context. The self-referential loop phenomenon — where the sculpture responds to its own electronic noise — is aesthetically interesting in its own right and may deserve deliberate exploration rather than suppression.
