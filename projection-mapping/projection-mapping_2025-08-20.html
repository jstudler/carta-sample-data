Showed the resin-embedded LED prototype at the department's open studio event last Friday. About 40 visitors over the evening. Some reflections:

The piece was a 30cm curved panel with 72 embedded LEDs, responding to proximity and ambient sound. It sat on a white pedestal at chest height in a partially darkened room.

What worked:

  • The glow-from-within effect was immediately captivating. Several visitors described it as "alive" or "breathing."
  • The proximity response created a natural, intuitive interaction — people instinctively moved their hands toward the panel and were rewarded with subtle color shifts.

What didn't work:

  • The sound response was confusing. When someone spoke loudly, the light pulsed in a way that seemed random rather than responsive. Need better mapping.
  • In the partially lit room, the panel competed with the overhead spotlights. The piece needs either full darkness or much brighter LEDs.

A deeper question emerged from conversations with visitors: several people asked whether the light patterns were "programmed" or "generated." When I explained that the patterns emerged from sensor data processed through filtering algorithms, they seemed simultaneously impressed and slightly disappointed — as though the magic diminished when the mechanism was explained. This raises interesting questions about transparency and enchantment in responsive art.
