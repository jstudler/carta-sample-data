Set up a series of projection tests using a short-throw projector aimed at curved plaster forms. The goal was to evaluate how well projection mapping works on non-planar surfaces compared to embedded LEDs.

Observations:

  • On convex surfaces, the projection stretches and distorts predictably — this can be corrected with software warping (used projection mapping software), but the correction is specific to the exact projector position.
  • On concave surfaces, the results are much more interesting. Light pools in the concavity and creates a sense of depth that embedded LEDs cannot achieve.
  • Shadow is a major factor. Any object or person between the projector and the sculpture casts shadows that break the illusion. This is both a limitation and an opportunity — viewer shadows could become part of the interaction.
  • Ambient light is the enemy of projection. The plaster forms need to be in a darkened space for projected light to read clearly. Embedded LEDs work in any lighting condition.

I am increasingly convinced that the two techniques serve different purposes. Embedded LEDs are for objects that glow from within — intimate, self-contained, portable. Projected light is for site-specific installations where the architecture of the space is part of the work.
