The washi paper diffusion technique discovered during the resin comparison has turned into something I did not expect.

When embedded between LED strips and the outer resin surface, the paper fibers create a diffusion pattern that is fundamentally different from the smooth, even glow of plain resin. Individual fibers catch and scatter light directionally, creating a subtle texture that shifts as the viewer moves around the piece. The effect is reminiscent of light filtering through cloud cover — organic, unpredictable, alive.

The problem is reproducibility. Each sheet of washi paper has a unique fiber structure, which means every casting produces a slightly different diffusion pattern. In one sense this is wonderful — each piece becomes truly unique. In another sense it makes it impossible to predict the visual result of a new casting, and impossible to replace a damaged panel with an identical one.

I am also uncertain about the long-term stability of paper embedded in resin. Paper is [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopy) — will it absorb moisture through micro-cracks in the resin over years? Will the fibers degrade under continuous LED illumination? There is very little published research on embedding natural fibers in epoxy for optical applications.

This technique produces the most beautiful results of anything in this project, but I cannot yet determine whether it is suitable for exhibition-grade work. It sits in an uncomfortable space between controlled fabrication and uncontrollable craft.
