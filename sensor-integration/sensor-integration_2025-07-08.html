--- title: Sensor Drift Over Extended Exhibitions date: 2025-07-08 topic: sensor-integration category: research type: imponderable width: 2 ---

An unresolved problem that I keep encountering: sensor drift over multi-week exhibition periods.

The ENV-340 temperature sensor drifts by approximately 0.3°C over 30 days. This is within the manufacturer's specification, but it means that the mapping from temperature to light color gradually shifts over the course of an exhibition. In the first week, the sculpture might glow warm amber at 22°C; by the fourth week, the same temperature produces a slightly different hue.

Is this a problem or a feature? The drift is imperceptible on any given day, but a visitor who returns after three weeks might notice that the sculpture "feels different." In one sense, this mirrors the way our own perception shifts over time — we never experience the same artwork the same way twice. In another sense, it undermines the reproducibility of the aesthetic experience.

Possible approaches:

  • Periodic recalibration against a reference sensor (adds complexity and a maintenance requirement)
  • Accept the drift as part of the work's temporal character
  • Use relative rather than absolute sensor values, so the mapping adapts to the current baseline

I have not resolved this question and I suspect it may not have a single correct answer. Each approach implies a different philosophical stance on what a responsive artwork is and what consistency means in a time-based practice.
